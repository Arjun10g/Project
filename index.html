Data Descriptives

The variables Q1 through Q32 were statements rated on a five point scale where 1=Never or very rarely true, 2=Rarely true, 3= Sometimes true, 4= Often true, 5=Very often or always true (-1=did not select an answer).

The dataset consists of 1071 observations and 35 variables with variables 1-32 comprising of information from the 32 items in the Questionnaire. The next two variables contained demographic information about gender and age. The final variable was a self report item on an individual's percieved accuray of the answers on the Questionnaire.

We removed 78 observations due to missing data based on the 32 items. The final sample was 993.

NOTE: The dataset is open-source is freely available on the internet.

The dataset is available here = http://openpsychometrics.org/_rawdata/

The authors in the paper attempted to collate humor related questions and group them into factors that best relate humor to psychosocial well being. They proposed a new multidimensional measure to assess their proposed factors.

Analysis Plan

We split the dataset into two samples for EFA and CFA

The sample size for the EFA subset was 497, whereas the sample size for the CFA subset was 496. The samples were split at random. All analysis was performed using R software.

EFA

The goal for the EFA was to discover the underlying factor structure for the test items and to further examine how well current theory holds up.

CFA

For the CFA, we aimed to confirm our factor structure (determined using EFA) and to further examine measurement invariance across age/gender.

Sequential Model Testing

Model fit statistics for the two most likely models were compared. Furthermore, we performed a likelhood ratio test and found that a five factor model was a significantly better fit compared to a four factor model.

Factor Rotation

The final step with regards to EFA analysis involved finding an interpretable solution for the factor loadings. In order to achieve this we rotated our factor solution using Oblimin rotation.

Choosing the Oblimin Weight

The decision to choose the gamma weight for the oblimin rotation is highly arbitary. Since our intended goal was to maximize row and column parsimony, we chose to automate this procedure. 56 different values of gamma were used to estimate average item complexities for each model. We discovered that a gamma of 0.2 led to the lowest item complexity.

Hence for the final model, we chose a gamma value of 0.2.

Model Output

The final model consisted of four factor solution. Oblimin rotation was applied to obtain an interpretable solution.

Multiple Group Analysis

We performed Multiple Group Analysis across Gender and Age to evaluate measurement invariance. The goal was to evaluate whether or not, under different conditions of observing and studying phenomena, measurements yield measures of the same attributes. We modelled the mean structure by including intercepts in the original model. Since invariance involves a hierarchy of models, testing for invariance required a multi-step procedure, testing more restrictive models at each step. The four conditions were as follows -

  1. Configural Invariance (No Restrictions)
  2. Weak Invariance (Constrained Factor Loadings)
  3. Strong Invariance (Constrained Factor Loadings and Intercepts)
  4. Strict Invariance (Constrained Factor Loadings, Intercepts and Variances)

Gender

Comparing the chi-squared change and fit indices for the all the models, we discovered that the strict invariance model performed similarly to the configural model.

Comparison to Current Literature

Data

Martin et. al (2003) : A subset of the data was selected for a PCA (n=485), PCA was cross-validated on another subset of participants (n= 452). CFA was conducted on all participant data (n=1195).

Current Analysis: Data was randomly split for EFA (n=497) and CFA (n=496) and cleaned to remove missing values.

Factor Retention

Martin et. al (2003): PCA using Varimax rotation was computed on 32 items. Examination of the scree plots, eigenvalues and factor loadings was used to make a decision that a 4-factor solution was optimal.

Current Analysis: Analysis: EFA on the 32 items was used to determine the factor structure using oblimin rotation, while selecting a gamma value that reduced item complexity the most. Decisions were made based on converging evidence: Revised Parallel Analysis, Comparison Data Approach, EKC, Sequential Model Testing.

Here, we decided to use a four-factor model, although a 5-factor model had slightly better fit indices. This is different from what was previously reported by the authors.

CFA

Martin et. al (2003): A four-factor model was tested, with the four scales as latent variables and eight items as indicators for each latent variable. Measurement error was assumed to be uncorrelated between items, while the four latent variables were allowed to covary. It was concluded that the model was a good fit of the data: “The results indicated a good fit to the data (CMIN/DF=3.37, GFI=.91, AGFI=.90, RMSEA=.048).” The Affiliative humor latent variable had a covariance of .34 with Self-enhancing humor, and the covariance between the Aggressive and Self-defeating humor latent variables was .36. Nonetheless, a higher order model was not explored.

Current Analysis: 28 items were selected for CFA. Decisions to remove items were made based on communalties, local independence violations, factor loadings and conceptual theory. With the exception of excluded items (6, 25 ,28 & 30), the same set of items loaded into the 4 latent variables describing the humor types. Affiliative and Self-enhancing humor loaded significantly onto Positive humor, whereas Aggressive and Self-defeating humor loaded significantly onto Negative humor.

We have shown that a higher order model describes the data well, but we found issues with model fit due to non-independence between questionnaire items. This was not previously described.

Group Invariance

Martin et. al (2003): Group differences were analysed by performing an ANOVA and t-tests for pairwise comparisons, on composite scores (the sum of the items the load into each humor type). The whole data was used for analysis (n=1195). The authors reported differences due to both sex and age.

Current Analysis: We performed a multiple group analysis. And discovered that there were no notable differences in the dataset (n=496) across gender and age.
